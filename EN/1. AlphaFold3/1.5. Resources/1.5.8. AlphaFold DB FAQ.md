# AlphaFold DB FAQ

[[Home|Home]] > [[EN/4. Datasets/4.3. AlphaFoldDB|AlphaFoldDB]] > FAQ
🇺🇦 [[UA/1. AlphaFold3/1.5. Ресурси/1.5.8. AlphaFold DB FAQ|Українська]]

This note consolidates the most practical user-facing answers from the official
`AlphaFold DB` FAQ, download pages, and entry-page metadata.

## What is AlphaFold DB, and what is it best for?

`AlphaFold DB` is a public database of predicted **protein** structures built
around `AlphaFold` models and distributed by `EMBL-EBI` and `Google DeepMind`.
In practice, it is best treated as a **fast structural hypothesis layer** for
single proteins, proteome-scale exploration, and downstream filtering.

It is especially useful when you need:

- a quick structural model for a known `UniProt` protein;
- residue-level confidence via `pLDDT`;
- large-scale download of predicted monomer structures;
- structural browsing before more expensive workflows such as `AF3`,
  docking, or local open-model inference.

It is **not** the right answer when your main target is a multimolecular
assembly, a protein-ligand complex, or a protein-nucleic-acid complex. For
those cases, `AF3`-style or other complex-aware pipelines are a better fit.

## How can I search for an entry?

The official site exposes search by:

- protein name;
- gene symbol;
- `UniProt` accession;
- organism;
- sequence-based search.

In practice, the most stable entry point is still the `UniProt` accession,
because it maps directly to a single structure page and avoids ambiguity from
gene naming.

## What information is shown on an entry page?

An entry page typically gives:

- summary and model-confidence panels;
- average `pLDDT` and residue-distribution buckets;
- domains and annotations;
- similar-protein views;
- metadata about the database version and model pipeline;
- licence, attribution, feedback, and disclaimer text.

This makes the entry page the fastest place to answer three practical
questions:

1. is there a prediction for this protein;
2. is the model locally confident enough to inspect;
3. should I download the structure or move to a more specialized workflow.

## What can I download?

| Level | What is available | Typical use |
|---|---|---|
| Single entry | one structure page per accession | inspect or export a specific protein |
| Species/proteome | bulk downloads for selected organisms | local proteome-scale analysis |
| `Swiss-Prot` | bulk curated subset | high-value practical subset |
| Full dataset | cloud-scale complete dump | local large-scale indexing and mining |

For bulk downloads, the main structure formats are `PDB` and `mmCIF`, usually
distributed as compressed archives.

## What happens for very long proteins?

For proteins longer than `2700 aa`, `AlphaFold DB` provides overlapping
fragments of length `1400 aa`. This matters because users sometimes expect to
find one single full-length structure page for giant proteins, but the database
may instead expose fragment models in the downloadable archives.

Practical implication:

- if a giant protein appears “missing” on the website, check the proteome
  archives first;
- fragment boundaries must be handled explicitly in local parsing or indexing;
- domain-level analysis is often safer than naive full-length interpretation.

## How should I interpret confidence on the site?

For day-to-day use, start with `pLDDT`:

- high `pLDDT` supports a locally reliable fold hypothesis;
- low `pLDDT` means “handle with caution”, not automatically “the whole model
  is useless”;
- domain arrangement and inter-region confidence may require additional views
  such as `PAE`.

For deeper interpretation of confidence, move from the website FAQ layer to the
dedicated notes on `pLDDT`, `lDDT`, and `Confidence Scores`.

## Can I use AlphaFold DB data commercially?

Yes. The official site states that the data are available for **academic and
commercial use** under `CC-BY-4.0`.

In practice, that means:

- you can use predictions in research and product workflows;
- you still need attribution and citation;
- you should also read the site disclaimer and `EMBL-EBI` terms of use.

## What should I cite?

The current practical rule is:

- cite the database paper itself;
- cite the relevant prediction source publication;
- if a structure page or dataset collection names a more specific publication,
  cite that as well.

For most standard `UniProt`-based `AlphaFold DB` entries, the two core
references are the `AlphaFold DB` resource paper and the original `AlphaFold`
paper.

## What is the official disclaimer?

The site explicitly frames these structures as **theoretical modelling** rather
than experimental truth. They are not a substitute for professional medical
advice, diagnosis, or treatment.

For practical work, that means:

- do not treat a prediction as experimental confirmation;
- validate high-stakes claims independently;
- separate “good structural hypothesis” from “biologically verified mechanism”.

## When is AlphaFold DB enough, and when do I need AF3 or another workflow?

| Goal | Use AlphaFold DB | Use AF3 / open complex-aware workflow |
|---|---|---|
| Single protein fold hypothesis | yes | usually unnecessary |
| Proteome-scale browsing | yes | too expensive |
| Bulk monomer download | yes | unnecessary |
| Protein-ligand complex | no | yes |
| Protein-RNA / protein-DNA complex | no | yes |
| Custom multimolecular assembly | no | yes |
| Domain or disorder triage by confidence | yes | sometimes later |

The simplest rule is: if your question is mostly about a **single protein
chain**, check `AlphaFold DB` first. If your question is about **interactions,
ligands, nucleic acids, or new multimolecular context**, move to `AF3`-like
systems or another complex-aware predictor.

## Feedback and support

The official entry pages point users to:

- in-page feedback buttons for structure-specific feedback;
- `alphafold@deepmind.com` for questions not covered in the FAQ;
- `alphafolddb@ebi.ac.uk` for website bugs or database-interface feedback.
